Part 1 — Waves

🌊 Intro to Waves

What exactly is a wave? Simply put, it's how energy travels from A to B.

Imagine doing a "Mexican Wave" in a stadium. You stand up and sit down. You don't run across the stadium, but the "wave" travels all the way around! This is what all waves do:

📖 Definition — Wave
A wave is a means of transferring energy by oscillations (vibrations) from one place to another, without transferring matter.

The Two Types of Waves

Waves fall into two categories depending on how they vibrate compared to the direction they are traveling.

Transverse Waves
The vibration is perpendicular (at right angles) to the direction the wave travels.

Example: Light waves, Water ripples, A shaken rope.
Longitudinal Waves
The vibration is parallel to the direction the wave travels. They squish together and pull apart.

Example: Sound waves.
🧠 Memory Trick: Think of a slinky. If you wiggle it up and down, that's a Transverse wave. If you push and pull it directly forward and back, that's a Longitudinal wave!
Part 1 — Waves

📏 Properties & Interference

The anatomy of a wave and what happens when they collide.

Wave Anatomy

  • Amplitude: The maximum distance the wave moves from its resting position (how tall the wave is). Determines loudness in sound!
  • Wavelength (λ): The distance from one crest (top) to the next crest. Measured in metres.
  • Frequency (f): The number of waves that pass a point every second. Measured in Hertz (Hz).
⚠️ Formula — Wave Speed
c = fλ
c = Speed of the wave (m/s)
f = Frequency (Hz)
λ = Wavelength (m)

Interference

What happens when two waves meet? They interfere with each other!

Constructive Interference
When two waves meet and their crests line up perfectly. They add together to make one bigger wave (larger amplitude).
Destructive Interference
When two waves meet and the crest of one lines up with the trough (bottom) of another. They cancel each other out. Noise-cancelling headphones use this!
Part 1 — Waves

🚨 Standing Waves & Doppler Effect

When waves get trapped, or when the source starts moving.

Stationary (Standing) Waves

If you pluck a guitar string, the wave travels to the end, reflects back, and interferes with itself. This creates a pattern that looks like it's standing still.

  • Nodes: The points that don't move at all (destructive interference).
  • Antinodes: The points that bounce up and down the most (constructive interference).
⚠️ Exam Rule: The distance between two consecutive nodes is always half a wavelength (λ/2)! The distance from a node to an antinode is λ/4.

The Doppler Effect

Have you ever noticed how an ambulance siren sounds high-pitched as it drives toward you, but suddenly drops to a lower pitch as it drives away? That's the Doppler Effect!

📖 Definition — The Doppler Effect
The apparent change in the frequency of a wave due to the relative motion between the source of the wave and the observer.

Why does this happen? As the ambulance moves towards you, it "catches up" to its own sound waves, squishing them together. Squished waves = shorter wavelength = higher frequency = higher pitch. When it drives away, the waves get stretched out behind it.

Part 2 — Sound

🔔 Sound & Resonance

How sound travels, and how it can be used to shatter glass.

Sound is a longitudinal wave. It travels by making the molecules in the air vibrate back and forth, passing the energy to the next molecule. This means sound needs a medium to travel through. It cannot travel through a vacuum (like space)!

Natural Frequency & Resonance

Every object (a guitar string, a wine glass, a bridge) has a specific frequency it naturally "wants" to vibrate at. This is its Natural Frequency.

📖 Definition — Resonance
Resonance is the transfer of energy between two bodies with the same natural frequency, causing the second body to vibrate with a large amplitude.

Example: If an opera singer sings a really loud note that perfectly matches the natural frequency of a wine glass, the sound waves force the glass to vibrate violently. It absorbs so much energy through resonance that it shatters!

Characteristics of a Musical Note

  • Pitch: Depends on the frequency of the wave.
  • Loudness: Depends on the amplitude of the wave.
  • Quality (Timbre): Depends on the number of harmonics (overtones) present. This is why a guitar and a piano playing the exact same note sound different.
Part 2 — Sound

🔊 Intensity & Decibels

How we measure the volume and power of sound.

When sound travels, it spreads out over an area. Sound Intensity is just a measure of how much power is hitting a certain area.

📖 Definition — Threshold of Hearing
The lowest sound intensity that the average human ear can detect at a frequency of 1000 Hz.

Humans can only hear sounds between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz. These are called our "frequency limits of audibility". Anything above 20,000 Hz is called ultrasound.

The Decibel Scale & dB(A)

Because human hearing covers such a massive range of sound intensities, we use a logarithmic scale called the Decibel (dB) scale.

The dB(A) Scale: The human ear isn't equally sensitive to all frequencies (we hear middle frequencies much better than really low or really high ones). The dB(A) scale is an adjusted scale that is weighted to match the response of the human ear. This is the scale used by health and safety authorities when measuring noise pollution.

⚠️ Exam Rule: If Sound Intensity doubles, the Sound Intensity Level increases by exactly 3 dB.
Part 2 — Sound

🎷 Harmonics & Pipes

How musical instruments actually create their notes.

When you pluck a string, it doesn't just vibrate as one big wave (the fundamental frequency). It also vibrates in halves, thirds, and quarters at the same time! These extra, higher frequencies are called harmonics. They are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency.

Sound in Pipes (Wind Instruments)

Wind instruments (like a flute or clarinet) create sound by setting up standing waves in the air inside the pipe.

Open Pipes
A pipe open at both ends (like a flute or tin whistle). It must have an antinode at both ends. They produce all harmonics.
Closed Pipes
A pipe closed at one end (like blowing across a bottle). The closed end forces a node, and the open end has an antinode. They only produce odd harmonics.
Part 3 — Light

🔦 Diffraction & Gratings

Proving that light behaves like a wave!

For a long time, scientists argued if light was a particle or a wave. Thomas Young proved it was a wave using his famous Slits experiment, because only waves can undergo diffraction and interference.

📖 Definition — Diffraction
The spreading out of a wave after passing through a gap or around an obstacle.

We use a Diffraction Grating (a piece of transparent material with thousands of tiny black lines engraved on it) to see this. When you shine a laser through it, the light spreads out from each slit and interferes, creating a pattern of bright dots on a screen.

⚠️ Formula — Diffraction Grating
nλ = d sinθ
n = Order of the image (1st dot, 2nd dot, etc)
λ (lambda) = Wavelength of the light
d = Distance between lines on the grating
θ (theta) = Angle from the center line to the dot
💡 Note: Monochromatic light means light of one wavelength (one colour) only, like a red laser.
Part 3 — Light

🌈 Polarisation & Colour

Sunglasses, rainbows, and how TVs trick your eyes.

Polarisation

A normal light wave vibrates in all directions (up, down, left, right). A polarised wave vibrates in one plane only. Polaroid sunglasses work by acting like vertical "slits" that block all the horizontal light (glare off the road or water).

🔥 Crucial Exam Fact: Polarisation proves that light is a transverse wave. Longitudinal waves (like sound) CANNOT be polarised!

Dispersion

White light is actually a mix of all the colours of the rainbow. Dispersion is the breaking up of white light into these constituent colours (producing a spectrum). A glass prism does this because different colours bend (refract) by different amounts. A rainbow is dispersion in nature!

Primary and Secondary Colours

  • Primary Colours: Red, Green, Blue. Mixing these creates all other colours. (This is how TV and phone screens work).
  • Secondary Colours: Cyan, Yellow, Magenta. Made by mixing two primary colours.
  • Complementary Colours: A primary and a secondary colour that, when mixed, give white light! (e.g., Blue + Yellow = White).
Part 3 — Light

📻 Electromagnetic Spectrum

Visible light is just a tiny sliver of reality.

There are many "invisible" light waves around us. Together, they form the Electromagnetic Spectrum. They all travel at the exact same speed in a vacuum (the speed of light: 3 × 10⁸ m/s) but have different wavelengths.

From shortest wavelength (highest energy) to longest wavelength (lowest energy):

Gamma Rays
Cancer treatment, very penetrating.
X-Rays
Photographing bones, detecting metal flaws.
Ultraviolet (UV)
Causes sunburn, detects forged money. Detected using fluorescent material.
Visible Light
What human eyes can see.
Infrared (IR)
Heat radiation. Used in night vision cameras and TV remotes. Detected with a thermometer.
Microwaves
Cooking, mobile phones, radar.
Radio Waves
Broadcasting TV and radio signals.

📐 Calculations

Step-by-step examples of typical Leaving Cert questions.

🧮 Example 1 — Wave Speed
Q: A wave has a frequency of 20 Hz. If the wavelength is 1.5 m, calculate the velocity of the wave.
Step 1 — Formula
c = fλ
Step 2 — Solve
c = (20)(1.5) = 30 m/s
🧮 Example 2 — Doppler Effect
Q: An ambulance siren emits a frequency of 750 Hz. When traveling towards an observer, the frequency detected is 820 Hz. Calculate the speed of the ambulance. (Speed of sound = 340 m/s).
Step 1 — Formula (Minus for approaching!)
f' = f(c) / (c - u)
Step 2 — Plug in values and cross multiply
820 = (750 × 340) / (340 - u)
820(340 - u) = 255000
278800 - 820u = 255000
Step 3 — Solve for u
23800 = 820u
u = 29 m/s
🧮 Example 3 — Tension in a String
Q: A stretched string vibrates at 205 Hz when the tension is 5 N. Calculate the fundamental frequency when tension is increased to 10 N.
Step 1 — The Rule
Frequency is proportional to the square root of tension (f ∝ √T).
Step 2 — Solve
Tension increased by a factor of 2 (from 5 to 10).
Therefore, frequency increases by a factor of √2.
f_new = √2 × 205 = 290 Hz.
🧮 Example 4 — Diffraction Grating
Q: A diffraction grating has 80 lines per mm. Find the grating constant 'd' in metres.
Step 1 — Find lines per meter
80 lines in 1 mm.
Therefore, 80,000 lines in 1 metre.
Step 2 — Calculate d
d = 1 / Number of lines per metre
d = 1 / 80000 = 1.25 × 10⁻⁵ m.

The mathematical toolkit for this unit.

Wave Equation
c = fλ

Frequency & Period
f = 1 / T
T=Periodic Time
The Doppler Effect
f' = f(c) / (c ± u)

nλ = d sinθ
n=order, d=grating constant, θ=angle
