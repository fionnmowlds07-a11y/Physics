Part 1 — Magnets & Fields

🧲 Magnets & Magnetic Fields

Understanding the basics of magnetism and the invisible fields that surround magnets.

Magnetic poles always exist in pairs, called the north pole and the south pole. A basic but essential rule to remember is: Like poles repel, unlike poles attract.

📖 Definition — Magnetic Field
A magnetic field is any region of space where magnetic forces can be felt.
🧠 Exam Tip: You must always remember that magnetic field lines go from the North Pole to the South Pole. When drawing them, don't forget the arrows!

Demonstrating Magnetic Fields

We can demonstrate the existence of a magnetic field using iron filings (which line up along the field) or small plotting compasses.

Part 1 — Magnets & Fields

🔄 Current & Magnetic Fields

Electricity and magnetism are linked! A flowing current creates a magnetic field.

When an electric current flows through a wire, a magnetic field is generated around it. The shape of this field depends on the shape of the wire.

Straight Wire
The magnetic field forms concentric circles around the wire.
Loop of Wire
The field lines are concentrated in the centre of the loop.
Solenoid
A coil of wire. The field is uniform inside and looks like a bar magnet on the outside.

Right Hand Grip Rule

To determine the direction of the magnetic field due to a current in a straight wire, we use the Right Hand Grip Rule:

  1. Grip the conductor in your right hand.
  2. Point your thumb in the direction of the current.
  3. Your fingers will curl in the direction of the magnetic field lines.
Part 2 — Current in a Magnetic Field

✋ Force on a Conductor

What happens when you put a wire carrying a current into a magnetic field? It experiences a force!

A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force. The size of this force depends on three things: the strength of the magnetic field, the size of the current, and the length of the conductor.

⚠️ Formula — Force on a Conductor
F = BIL
F = Force (Newtons, N)
B = Magnetic Flux Density (Tesla, T)
I = Current (Amperes, A)
L = Length of conductor in the field (metres, m)

Fleming’s Left Hand Rule

To find the direction of the force, we use Fleming's Left Hand Rule. Point your fingers at right angles to each other:

  • Thumb = Direction of Thrust (Force)
  • First Finger = Direction of magnetic Field (North to South)
  • Second Finger = Direction of Current
💡 Application: Devices that rely on the principle that a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force include the electric motor, the galvanometer, and the loudspeaker.
📖 Definition — The Ampere
A current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field experiences a force. Two parallel wires will exert a force on each other. This is the principle on which the definition of the ampere is based.
Part 2 — Current in a Magnetic Field

💨 Force on a Moving Charge

Individual moving charges (like electrons) also experience a force when they fly through a magnetic field.

Current is just flowing charge. So, if a wire carrying current experiences a force, an individual charged particle moving through a magnetic field will also experience a force.

⚠️ Formula — Force on a Moving Charge
F = qvB
F = Force (Newtons, N)
q = Charge of the particle (Coulombs, C)
v = Velocity of the particle (m/s)
B = Magnetic Flux Density (Tesla, T)

Because the force is always at right angles to the direction of motion (thanks to Fleming's Left Hand Rule), it acts as a centripetal force, causing the particle to move in a circular path!

Part 3 — Electromagnetic Induction

⚡ Induction & Magnetic Flux

If electricity can create magnetism, can magnetism create electricity? Yes!

📖 Definition — Electromagnetic Induction
Electromagnetic induction occurs when an EMF (electromotive force / voltage) is induced in a coil due to a changing magnetic flux.

Michael Faraday discovered that moving a magnet in and out of a coil of wire generates electricity. The needle on a connected galvanometer deflects, showing that an EMF is induced, which in turn produces a current.

Magnetic Flux

To calculate the induced EMF, we first need to understand Magnetic Flux. You can visualise this as the number of magnetic field lines passing through a given area.

⚠️ Formula — Magnetic Flux
Φ = BA
Φ (Phi) = Magnetic Flux (Webers, Wb)
B = Magnetic Flux Density (Tesla, T)
A = Cross-sectional area (m²)
Part 3 — Electromagnetic Induction

📜 Faraday's & Lenz's Laws

The two golden rules that govern how and why electricity is induced.

📖 Definition — Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction
Faraday's Law states that the magnitude of the induced EMF is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux.

Basically: if you move the magnet faster, or use a stronger magnet, or a coil with more turns, you get a larger voltage!

📖 Definition — Lenz's Law of Electromagnetic Induction
Lenz's Law states that the direction of the induced EMF is always such as to oppose the change producing it.

Lenz's law is essentially conservation of energy. If you push a North pole into a coil, the coil will induce a current that creates a North pole at that end to repel your magnet. You have to do work (push hard) to overcome this repulsion, and that work is converted into electrical energy!

Part 3 — Electromagnetic Induction

🔌 AC & Transformers

How we manipulate voltages for long-distance power transmission.

Mutual Induction

An alternating current (AC) in one coil creates a constantly changing magnetic field. If a second coil is placed nearby, this changing magnetic flux induces an alternating EMF in the second coil. This is called mutual induction.

📖 Definition — Mutual Induction
Mutual induction is the process where a changing current in one coil induces an EMF in a second nearby coil.

Transformers

Transformers use mutual induction to step up (increase) or step down (decrease) AC voltages. They only work with Alternating Current (AC) because direct current (DC) would create a steady magnetic field, which wouldn't induce an EMF.

⚠️ Formula — Transformer Equation
Vs / Vp = Ns / Np
Vs = Voltage in Secondary Coil
Vp = Voltage in Primary Coil
Ns = Number of turns in Secondary Coil
Np = Number of turns in Primary Coil

Why use transformers? High voltage is used when transmitting electricity over national grids because high voltage means low current, which vastly reduces heat loss in the wires (improving efficiency).

📐 Calculations

Step-by-step examples of typical Leaving Cert questions.

🧮 Example 1 — Force on a Conductor
Q: A conductor of length 50 cm is carrying a current of 5 A. It is placed at right angles to a magnetic field of flux density 3 T. Calculate the force on the conductor.
Step 1 — Identify variables and ensure correct units
L = 50 cm = 0.5 m
I = 5 A
B = 3 T
Step 2 — Use the formula F = BIL
F = (3)(5)(0.5)
F = 7.5 N
🧮 Example 2 — Magnetic Flux
Q: A square coil of side 5 cm lies perpendicular to a magnetic field of flux density 4.0 T. The coil consists of 200 turns of wire. What is the magnetic flux cutting the coil?
Step 1 — Calculate Area in m²
Side = 5 cm = 0.05 m
Area = 0.05 × 0.05 = 0.0025 m²
Step 2 — Apply flux formula Φ = BA
B = 4.0 T
Φ = (4.0)(0.0025) = 0.01 Wb
Note!
The formula Φ = BA calculates the flux for ONE turn. The question asks for the flux cutting the coil. For 200 turns, it's 200 × 0.01 = 2 Wb.
🧮 Example 3 — Transformer
Q: There are 150 turns in the primary coil of a transformer and 3000 turns in the secondary coil. Calculate the output voltage when 12 V AC is connected across the primary coil.
Step 1 — Identify variables
Np = 150
Ns = 3000
Vp = 12 V
Vs = ?
Step 2 — Rearrange and solve Vs / Vp = Ns / Np
Vs = (Ns / Np) × Vp
Vs = (3000 / 150) × 12
Vs = 20 × 12 = 240 V

📖 All Definitions

Learn these off by heart for the exam!

Magnetic Field
A magnetic field is any region of space where magnetic forces can be felt.
Magnetic Flux (Φ)
Magnetic flux is defined as the product of magnetic flux density and the cross-sectional area perpendicular to the direction of the magnetic flux density.
Electromagnetic Induction
Electromagnetic induction occurs when an emf is induced in a coil due to a changing magnetic flux.
Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction
The magnitude of the induced EMF is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux.
Lenz's Law of Electromagnetic Induction
The direction of the induced EMF is always such as to oppose the change producing it.
Mutual Induction
An alternating current in one coil results in an induced EMF in a second nearby coil.
Self Induction
A changing magnetic field in a coil induces an EMF in the coil itself that opposes the changing current.
